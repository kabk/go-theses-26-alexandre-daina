When Perception Devours Its Anchor

Scroll

The door opens.
The castle does not threaten.

It receives.

The first nights are quiet in a way that feels medicinal, not empty, but contained, as if sound itself has been filtered, slowed down, and redistributed across the thickness of the walls.
There are no sirens, no overlapping voices, no reflections multiplying his image from every surface.

Only stone, and sounds that arrive softened, delayed, and contained, as if filtered through the thickness of the walls.

Footsteps travel through corridors and return a fraction of a second later, slightly displaced, as if the space is responding rather than simply reflecting.
Doors close with a muted heaviness that seems to linger in the air longer than expected, and the wind, moving along the outer walls, produces a continuous low presence that never fully disappears, like a background layer that cannot be switched off.

Days begin to take shape through repetition.

Not imposed abruptly, but installed gradually, until they appear natural.

The psychologist interviews him.

Interviews occur in a fixed room, always at the same time, where the psychologist asks Tristan to describe what he notices, not only what he feels, but how he perceives, how long things last, how space behaves around him.
The questions are simple, but they insist on detail.

Notes are taken.

The routine feels stable.

Day 2:
Tristan walks around the perimeter of the castle.
Rooms are explained.
The logic of the space is introduced, not as architecture, but as something to inhabit and observe.

Morning walks along the wall, where the landscape extends without interruption.
Reading in the south chamber, where light enters at a precise angle and remains constant.
Meals at fixed hours, always in the same room.

Evening silence.

At first, the silence feels restorative, a space where nothing demands him, where no one is watching, where his attention no longer needs to anticipate interruption.

But gradually, that absence begins to shift. Silence does not remain empty.

It becomes resonant.

Without external interaction, perception does not diminish; it redirects, folding inward, expanding into areas that previously remained unnoticed.
There are no external gazes to anticipate, no reactions to adjust to, no unpredictable interruptions forcing him outward.

So he begins anticipating himself.

He notices how his footsteps echo, not only that they echo, but how long the echo lasts, how it changes depending on the corridor,
how it slightly delays, as if it hesitates before returning.

He repeats the same walk at different times of day.

He compares.
He listens.

He times the church bell in the village below. Precise intervals.

The regularity becomes reassuring.

Then absorbing, not just reassuring through its regularity, but beginning to occupy his attention completely, drawing him into its precision and demanding to be tracked, measured, and understood.
He wonders who maintains it, who ensures its consistency, whether someone is there or whether it continues independently.

Small observations accumulate, and what begins as attention slowly becomes a system of measurement.

He starts mapping space through duration.

How long it takes to cross a corridor.
How many steps from one door to another.
How sound shifts between rooms, sometimes absorbed, sometimes amplified.
How shadows move across the walls during the afternoon, not randomly, but following a pattern that feels almost intentional.

The interviews become less frequent.
Then they stop.
The absence is not announced.

It simply occurs.

Instead, written notes begin appearing beneath his door.
Continue observation.
Reduce village contact.

The phrasing remains familiar, but something in the tone has shifted, less conversational, more directive.

The handwriting is similar.
But more vertical.

More controlled.

He assumes fatigue, or a shift toward efficiency,
a more controlled and impersonal way of maintaining the process.

But the difference persists.

He begins noticing that report entries describe emotional states before he consciously experiences them, as if the writing precedes the feeling rather than records it.

Moments appear documented that he does not remember describing. Sentences that feel like his, but not entirely.
At the same time, perception begins to slip.
Not dramatically.
Subtly.

A figure at the end of a corridor that seems present until he approaches and finds nothing. A voice resembling a friend, heard briefly, without a clear origin.

Reflections that appear slightly delayed, as if they follow rather than mirror.
These are not events.
They are discrepancies.

In the west wing, he finds a notebook with his name embossed on the cover, placed in a room he does not remember entering before.

Inside:

Day 25:
Subject displays perceptual doubling.

It is Day 22.

He turns the pages.
The handwriting resembles his own.

Only steadier, more detached, as if written by someone observing him from a position without emotion or urgency.

He does not remember writing any of it. The authority of the psychologist that accompanied him on the train begins dissolving, not only through absence, but through uncertainty.

Was the psychologist external?
Or part of the same system he is now inside?

The castle no longer feels supervised.
It feels self-sustaining.
He checks the gravel outside for signs of movement.
None.

He checks the phone line.
Disconnected.

He searches for traces of the clinic in Paris.
No record.

The structure remains.
The observer does not.

He walks to the church again, drawn by the regularity of the bell, by the idea that something outside the castle might still function independently of him.

He sits beneath the vertical architecture, waiting.

The voice disperses into space.
It does not reach him in the way he expects.

Nothing transfers.

He realizes something slowly, almost reluctantly.

In Paris, perception was unstable, but it was shared. It was shaped by others, interrupted, corrected, constantly negotiated.

Here, it is closed. There is no interruption.
No counterweight.
No external gaze to break the loop.

The castle does not introduce hallucination as something new.

It amplifies awareness until awareness bends back onto itself.

Corridors feel longer in the evening, not because they change, but because his sense of duration stretches within them.
Reflections lag by a fraction, barely noticeable, but enough to disturb alignment. Sound detaches slightly from its source, arriving too early or too late, creating a gap that cannot be resolved.

Perception is no longer orienting.
It is generating.

The observer and the observed collapse into the same position.
There is no supervising authority.
The experiment continues.

One day, he ascends the donjon tower of the castle.

The staircase spirals inward, tightening as it rises, compressing movement into repetition. At the highest point, the structure opens.

Wind surrounds him, It is black outside,There is no guardrail.

The horizon expands in every direction, uninterrupted, without reference points.
For a moment, everything appears stable.

Then something shifts.

At the edge of his vision, a movement.

He turns.
Nothing.

But the sensation remains, not outside, but just behind him, delayed, as if something follows a fraction of a second too late.

He steps forward.It follows.

He begins to understand.
Not as a thought, but as a pressure.

It moves as he moves.
It stops as he stops.

Closer now.
Too close.

He turns again, faster. And for a brief instant, he sees it. Not a monster, but a face.

His face, misaligned, lagging, arriving too late to be him, yet too precise to be anything else. The second Tristan.
No longer above him, but behind him, advancing.

The bell rings, precise.

The presence advances. There is no space left to correct, no distance left to re-align.
Only the edge. Only the final misalignment.

He moves to escape it, or to escape himself.
His foot slips.
The body disappears into the open.

Silence follows.
