The Train as Suspension

Scroll

The journey to the castle begins by train.

The train is neither city nor countryside.
It is a space in-between, a suspension where belonging temporarily dissolves.

Tristan sits opposite the psychologist in a quiet carriage as Paris dissolves into industrial edges, then fields, then long horizontal stretches that seem to extend without interruption, flattening the world into distance.

The psychologist speaks in calm intervals, outlining a method.

Isolation, he explains, can reduce external triggers.
Routine will be implemented.
Sessions will be maintained.

The authority feels reassuring.

There is structure in this transfer, a sense that something is being guided, contained, and observed from the outside.

For the first time in weeks, Tristan feels accompanied without being scrutinized. The presence of the psychologist absorbs part of the doubling, softening the split between the one who lives and the one who observes.

They pass through tunnels.

For a few seconds at a time, reflection replaces landscape in the window glass. Tristan sees himself layered over darkness.

He notices it immediately.
And this time, he decides to look away.

Outside, the countryside unfolds in wide, uninterrupted planes.
Villages appear briefly and disappear again, leaving behind stretches of emptiness that feel increasingly uniform.

He feels something shift. Not relief.

But thinning.

In Paris, hyper-awareness was constantly activated and shaped by others, by reactions, by subtle feedback loops that forced him to adjust, even if uncomfortably.
That constant external reference, though exhausting, provided a form of orientation.

Here, that vigilance disappears.

No one looks at him.
No one responds.

The horizon stretches too far.

The silence between places expands.

Without realizing it, Tristan begins paying attention differently.

The rhythm of the train on the tracks becomes more present, repetitive, almost hypnotic. A faint electrical hum runs above.

The seat vibrates subtly beneath him, transmitting movement directly into his body.

His awareness spreads into the quiet space.

He asks:
“What happens if the silence becomes louder?”

The psychologist responds:

“Then we observe it.”

Observe.

The word stabilizes him, because here observation is positioned as external and structured, guided by another, unlike hyper-awareness, which trapped him inside his own continuous self- monitoring without distance.

There will be supervision: regular conversations, structured check-ins, a presence that remains external.

There will be documentation: written records, traces of what is happening.
There will be someone measuring what he cannot measure himself.

The train slows.

The station is nearly empty, a small rural platform that feels disproportionately large in its silence.
Night has already settled.

A single overhead lamp casts a narrow circle of light.
They step onto the platform.
A butler waits beside an old car.

The castle appears gradually.
Not imposing. Contained.

Stone absorbs light rather than reflecting it.
Most windows remain dark.

In the distance, a church bell marks the hour.
The sound lingers slightly longer than expected.

The car stops.

The psychologist exits first and walks ahead without hesitation.
Authority precedes him.

For a brief moment, Tristan feels safe.
